# MatchaMD: The Definitive US Residency Match & Strategy Guide for IMGs
*A Comprehensive Capstone Guide for International Medical Graduates (IMGs)*

Applying for a medical residency in the United States as an International Medical Graduate (IMG) is a monumental task. From navigating foreign clinical settings to managing multi-layered bureaucratic processes across the Educational Commission for Foreign Medical Graduates (ECFMG), the Association of American Medical Colleges (AAMC), and the National Resident Matching Program (NRMP), the journey requires meticulous, strategic, and early planning.

This guide acts as your capstone roadmap, compiling authoritative data, timeline strategies, and actionable advice to help you build a competitive application, select the right programs, design an optimal Rank Order List, and successfully secure a U.S. residency position.

---

## 1. The US Residency Match: An Overview

The United States residency application and matching process is unique. Unlike job markets where employers hire candidates directly, U.S. graduate medical education (GME) utilizes a centralized, computerized matching algorithm to pair applicants with residency training programs.

### The Core Systems
To navigate the Match, you must register with and utilize three distinct organizations:
1. **ECFMG (Educational Commission for Foreign Medical Graduates):** A division of Intealth, ECFMG acts as the clearinghouse for all international medical students and graduates. ECFMG verifies your credentials, administers the eligibility to take the USMLE exams, evaluates your clinical skills via the ECFMG Pathways, and issues the ECFMG Certification required to enter residency. It also serves as your designated "Dean's Office" for uploading letters of recommendation, medical school transcripts, and your Medical School Student Performance Evaluation (MSPE).
2. **AAMC (Association of American Medical Colleges):** The AAMC operates the **Electronic Residency Application Service (ERAS)**. This centralized portal is where you build your professional profile, upload your personal statement, select residency programs, submit your letters of recommendation, and formally transmit your application to programs.
3. **NRMP (National Resident Matching Program):** The NRMP operates the **Main Residency Match**. You must register separately with the NRMP. After application submission and interviews, both applicants and program directors submit a preferred rank list to the NRMP. A Nobel Prize-winning computerized matching algorithm then matches the preferences of applicants with those of residency programs to produce a single, legally binding outcome.

### How the Match Algorithm Works
The Match utilizes the **Roth-Peranson Algorithm**. It is "applicant-proposing," meaning it prioritizes the preferences of the applicant over the preferences of the residency program. The algorithm begins with an applicant's first-choice program and attempts to place them there. If that program has open slots, the applicant is provisionally matched. If the program is filled with higher-ranked applicants, the algorithm moves to the applicant’s second choice, and so on. No match is finalized until the algorithm completes its run, meaning you should always rank programs in order of your *true preference*, not where you think you have the best chance of matching.

### Match Week and SOAP
Each year in mid-March, "Match Week" begins. 
* **Monday of Match Week:** Applicants receive an email informing them *whether* they matched, but not *where*.
* **Monday through Thursday:** Programs with unfilled positions and unmatched or partially matched applicants participate in the **Supplemental Offer and Acceptance Program (SOAP)**. This is a rapid, multi-round electronic system designed to fill remaining residency slots.
* **Friday of Match Week (Match Day):** At 12:00 PM Eastern Time, matched applicants across the globe simultaneously receive their Match results, revealing the specific program where they will train for the next three to seven years.

## 2. Match Rates for IMGs: Current Data and Trends

Understanding the numbers is critical for building a realistic Match strategy. The residency landscape has become increasingly competitive, but international applicants remain a vital pillar of the U.S. healthcare workforce. 

In the **2026 Main Residency Match**, the NRMP recorded the largest match in history, offering a record **44,344 total positions**.

### PGY-1 Match Rate Trends by Applicant Type
The performance of IMGs in the Match is evaluated using two distinct categories: U.S. Citizen IMGs (typically U.S. nationals who went abroad for medical school, often to the Caribbean) and Non-U.S. Citizen IMGs (foreign nationals requiring visa sponsorship).

| Applicant Category | 2024 Match Rate | 2025 Match Rate | 2026 Match Rate |
| :--- | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| **U.S. Citizen IMGs** | 62.6% | 67.8% | **70.0%** |
| **Non-U.S. Citizen IMGs** | 57.4% | 58.0% | **56.4%** |

*Source: NRMP Main Residency Match Results and Data Reports (2024, 2025, 2026).*

### Key takeaways from recent trends:
* **U.S. Citizen IMGs** have seen a steady, positive trajectory, hitting a **five-year high of 70.0%** in 2026. This is largely driven by structured clinical rotations in the U.S. offered by major international medical schools, strong English communication skills, and the absence of visa sponsorship requirements.
* **Non-U.S. Citizen IMGs** face a tougher landscape. After rising slightly to 58.0% in 2025, the match rate declined to **56.4% in 2026**. This decline highlights the tightening of visa-sponsored positions, the challenges of securing high-quality clinical encounters, and the transition of USMLE Step 1 to a pass/fail-only reporting system, which places extra emphasis on Step 2 CK.
* **Visa Sponsorship Rates:** Non-U.S. IMGs requiring visa sponsorship matched at a rate of **54.4%** in 2026, which represents a five-year low.

### Specialty Competitiveness for IMGs
Historically, IMGs match predominantly into primary care and supportive specialties. The following lists detail the general breakdown of specialties based on IMG friendliness:

* **High IMG Friendliness (Highest Match Rates & Volumes):**
  * Internal Medicine (Categorical)
  * Family Medicine
  * Pediatrics
  * Pathology
  * Neurology
  * Psychiatry

* **Moderate IMG Friendliness (Competitive but Attainable with Outstanding Profiles):**
  * General Surgery (Categorical)
  * Anesthesiology
  * Emergency Medicine
  * Physical Medicine & Rehabilitation (PM&R)
  * Obstetrics and Gynecology (Ob/Gyn)

* **Low IMG Friendliness (Extremely Competitive / Low Match Rates):**
  * Dermatology
  * Orthopaedic Surgery
  * Plastic Surgery
  * Otolaryngology (ENT)
  * Neurological Surgery
  * Ophthalmology

## 3. Building a Competitive Application

With USMLE Step 1 now being entirely pass/fail, your application must stand out through a mosaic of achievements. Program directors look at a holistic representation of your medical knowledge, clinical competence, communication skills, and adaptability.

### USMLE Scores
* **Step 1:** While scored Step 1 results are no longer issued, securing a **"Pass" on your first attempt** is an absolute prerequisite. A failed attempt on Step 1 acts as an automatic filter for many residency programs.
* **Step 2 CK (Clinical Knowledge):** This is now the **single most important numerical metric** on your application. For IMGs, a stellar Step 2 CK score is crucial to offset the lack of a U.S. institutional name on your diploma.
  * *Target Score:* To be highly competitive in primary care (Internal Medicine, Pediatrics, Family Medicine), you should aim for a **240+**. For highly competitive specialties (e.g., General Surgery or Anesthesiology), you must target a **250+ or 260+**.
* **Step 3:** Taking and passing USMLE Step 3 *before* submitting your ERAS application is a major advantage for non-U.S. IMGs. It proves you are clinically competent, reduces the risk of residency training interruptions, and makes you immediately eligible for an H-1B visa (which requires a Step 3 passing score) instead of a J-1 visa.

### Medical School Performance and MSPE
Your Medical School Student Performance Evaluation (MSPE), or Dean's Letter, must be uploaded to ERAS. While grading systems vary internationally, programs value comments regarding your professionalism, clinical work ethic, and any peer rankings or awards.

### Letters of Recommendation (LoRs)
You can submit up to four Letters of Recommendation per program in ERAS. For an IMG, **at least three letters should come from U.S. physicians** who have directly supervised your clinical work in a U.S. healthcare setting.
* **Quality over Title:** A highly detailed letter from an attending physician who worked with you daily in a community clinic carries significantly more weight than a generic, one-paragraph letter signed by a world-renowned Department Chair who barely knows your name.
* **Specialty-Specific LoRs:** Your letters must be tailored to the specialty you are applying to. If you are applying to both Internal Medicine and Family Medicine, secure distinct letters for each.

### Research and Publications
Research experience is highly valued, particularly in academic programs and competitive specialties.
* Focus on peer-reviewed publications, case reports, and oral or poster presentations.
* Listing active U.S.-based research fellowships can act as a powerful bridge, providing you with academic networking opportunities and U.S.-based recommendation letters.

### Volunteer Work and Leadership
Residency programs seek well-rounded, compassionate physicians. Volunteer experiences in underserved areas, leadership roles in medical student organizations, and participation in community health campaigns demonstrate your dedication to service and collaboration.

## 4. US Clinical Experience (USCE): Why It Matters

US Clinical Experience (USCE) is not just a line item on your CV; for international medical graduates, it is the bridge of trust. It demonstrates to program directors that you are familiar with the complexities of the U.S. healthcare system—including electronic health records (EHR), interdisciplinary care teams, patient-centered communication, and medical-legal frameworks.

### Types of USCE and Their Value
Not all USCE is viewed equally. Understanding the hierarchy of experiences is critical when investing your time and money.

```
  [HIGH VALUE]   Hands-On Electives (For Medical Students)
       │         Sub-Internships at U.S. Teaching Hospitals
       ▼
  [MODERATE]     Hands-On Externships (For Graduates)
       │         Hands-On Clinical Rotations in Community Clinics
       ▼
  [LOW VALUE]    Observerships (Passive observation, no patient contact)
```

1. **Hands-On Clinical Electives (Highest Value):** These are available **only to current medical students** (before graduation). You function at the level of a U.S. medical student, taking histories, performing physical exams, presenting patients, and documenting encounters.
2. **Sub-Internships (Very High Value):** An intensive hands-on clinical experience where you assume the responsibilities of an intern under close supervision. This is highly prized by academic program directors.
3. **Externships (High to Moderate Value):** Hands-on clinical experiences designed for **medical graduates**. While liability insurance and state licensing laws can make these harder to secure, they offer the hands-on clinical exposure that programs want to see.
4. **Observerships (Lowest Value):** These are strictly passive. You shadow an attending physician but are legally prohibited from touching patients, taking histories, or inputting data into the EHR. While better than nothing, a pure observership does not prove clinical competence and should be used primarily as a networking tool or to secure an introductory U.S. letter of recommendation.

### What Counts and What Doesn't
* **What Counts:** Any experience located in a U.S. clinic or hospital where you interact with U.S. patients, work under U.S.-licensed attendings, and participate in U.S. healthcare workflows.
* **What Doesn't Count:** Tele-rotations (virtual rotations done from your home country) have lost almost all credibility post-COVID. Program directors view these as passive online lectures rather than clinical experience. Similarly, observerships without any accompanying letter of recommendation or structured program carry minimal weight.

### Strategic Tips for Securing USCE
* **Start Early:** If you are a medical student, apply for university-affiliated electives 9–12 months in advance.
* **Leverage Alumni:** Reach out to graduates of your medical school who are currently training or practicing in the U.S. They are your best advocates for finding observerships or externships.
* **Third-Party Agencies:** Companies like AMOpportunities, Chicago Clerkships, and FMG Portal offer structured clinical rotations for a fee. While expensive, they guarantee placement and assist with malpractice insurance. Use them if you lack direct academic connections.

## 5. Choosing Your Specialty as an IMG

The single most common mistake made by IMGs is applying to a specialty without evaluating the underlying data. You must balance your passion with statistical reality.

### Specialty Analysis for IMGs
Let's analyze the match data across major specialties to help you categorize your options.

#### 1. Internal Medicine (Categorical)
* **Match Landscape:** The largest specialty in the Match and the primary gateway for IMGs. 
* **IMG Friendliness:** **Very High.** In the 2026 Match, thousands of IMG applicants matched into Internal Medicine programs, making up a significant portion of the total matched pool.
* **Strategy:** Highly accessible. Academic programs look for research, while community programs place a premium on high Step 2 CK scores and solid U.S. clinical experience.

#### 2. Family Medicine
* **Match Landscape:** Offers a large number of positions and focuses heavily on outpatient, preventative care, and community medicine.
* **IMG Friendliness:** **High.** Over 80% of Family Medicine programs regularly interview and rank IMGs.
* **Strategy:** Highlight your passion for primary care, preventive medicine, and long-term patient relationships. U.S. clinical experience in outpatient settings is crucial.

#### 3. Pediatrics
* **Match Landscape:** Highly structured, welcoming, and patient-centered.
* **IMG Friendliness:** **High.** Pediatrics consistently fills a substantial number of slots with IMGs.
* **Strategy:** Emphasize any international pediatric clinical experience, child advocacy, and strong interpersonal communication skills.

#### 4. Pathology
* **Match Landscape:** A diagnostic specialty that does not require direct patient care.
* **IMG Friendliness:** **Exceptional.** Historically, Pathology has one of the highest percentages of IMG residents (often over 30–40% of matched positions).
* **Strategy:** Requires a very specific CV. Programs expect to see direct pathology clinical electives, laboratory experience, and dedicated research or publications in pathology journals.

#### 5. Psychiatry
* **Match Landscape:** Highly competitive in recent years due to lifestyle appeal and growing awareness of mental health.
* **IMG Friendliness:** **Moderate.** While still accessible, the barrier to entry has risen significantly.
* **Strategy:** Exceptional communication skills and English proficiency are mandatory. Observerships or externships in psychiatric facilities are critical to demonstrate cultural competence in handling U.S. patient populations.

#### 6. General Surgery (Categorical)
* **Match Landscape:** Highly competitive with long training hours and demanding requirements.
* **IMG Friendliness:** **Moderate to Low.** While many IMGs match, they represent a small fraction of categorical positions. Many more match into Preliminary positions (one-year contracts that do not guarantee a full residency).
* **Strategy:** To match into a categorical General Surgery spot, you must possess outstanding Step 2 CK scores (typically 250+), extensive U.S. surgical clinical electives, and a strong track record of surgical research.

### Being Realistic: The "Backup" Specialty Strategy
If you are aiming for a moderately competitive specialty (e.g., General Surgery, Ob/Gyn, or Anesthesiology), it is highly recommended to apply to a backup specialty (e.g., Internal Medicine or Family Medicine) simultaneously.
* **Separate Applications:** If applying to two specialties, construct **two entirely different ERAS profiles**. Your personal statement, chosen letters of recommendation, and highlighted experiences must be tailored to each specialty. A program director in Family Medicine will immediately discard your application if your personal statement talks about your lifelong dream of becoming a general surgeon.

## 6. Program Selection Strategy

Applying to the wrong programs is a waste of financial resources and decreases your chances of matching. You must research programs individually and build a targeted application list.

### Determining the Number of Applications
For IMGs, the old advice of applying to "30 to 40 programs" is outdated. In the current era, IMGs typically apply to **80 to 150+ programs per specialty** to maximize their chances of receiving interviews. However, due to AAMC's new fee structure, you must be highly strategic about how you allocate these applications.

### The AAMC ERAS Fee Structure
AAMC utilizes a simplified residency application fee structure:
* **1 to 30 Programs (per specialty):** Flat rate of **$11 per program**.
* **31 or more Programs (per specialty):** Flat rate of **$30 per program** for every application beyond the 30th.

*Example:* Applying to 100 Internal Medicine programs will cost:
* First 30 programs: $30 \times \$11 = \$330$
* Next 70 programs: $70 \times \$30 = \$2,100$
* **Total ERAS Fee:** **$2,430** (plus taxes and USMLE transcript transmission fees).

Because fees increase substantially after the 30th program, you must ensure that every program on your list is truly realistic.

### Identifying IMG-Friendly Programs
Do not blind-apply. Use these databases and methods to filter your list:
1. **FREIDA (AMA Residency Database):** The American Medical Association’s FREIDA database allows you to search for programs and filter by the percentage of IMGs currently in training. Look for programs where **at least 15–20% of the current residents are IMGs**.
2. **Residency Explorer (AAMC-backed tool):** Allows you to compare your Step 2 CK scores and research history with matched applicants at specific programs.
3. **Match A Resident:** A paid database that compiles historical IMG data, visa sponsorship information, and specific program requirements (e.g., USMLE cutoffs and time-since-graduation limits).
4. **Program Websites:** Always double-check a program's official website before applying. Look for their explicit requirements regarding:
   * *Cutoff Scores:* Do they require a minimum score on USMLE Step 2 CK?
   * *Time Since Graduation (YoG):* Many programs have strict cutoffs (e.g., graduation within the last 2, 3, or 5 years). If you graduated 7 years ago, do not apply to a program with a strict 3-year cutoff.
   * *Visa Sponsorship:* Ensure they explicitly sponsor J-1 or H-1B visas if you require them.

### Geographic Strategy
Residency programs often exhibit geographic bias.
* **IMG-Friendly States:** Historically, certain states have a high density of IMG-friendly programs due to high patient volumes, diverse demographics, and state funding rules. These include **New York, New Jersey, Michigan, Ohio, Pennsylvania, Illinois, and Texas**.
* **Regional Preferences in ERAS:** ERAS allows you to signal geographic preferences. Be honest and strategic. If you have family in the Northeast, signaling the Middle Atlantic division can significantly increase your interview conversion rate in New York, New Jersey, and Pennsylvania.

## 7. Interview Season

Earning an interview invitation means your paper application has passed the filters. At this stage, you are on equal footing with every other interviewee. The interview is about personality, fit, communication, and professionalism.

### Virtual Interview Environment
Almost all initial residency interviews are conducted virtually (via Zoom, WebEx, or Thalamus). You must treat your virtual interview space as a professional studio:
* **Equipment:** Invest in a 1080p external webcam, a dedicated ring light placed behind your camera, and a reliable external microphone.
* **Background:** Set up in a quiet, well-lit room. Use a professional, clean, non-distracting background (a plain wall, a tidy bookshelf, or a professional neutral virtual background if your physical space is cluttered).
* **Internet Connection:** Use a wired ethernet cable instead of Wi-Fi to eliminate the risk of call drops or lag. Have a mobile hotspot ready as a backup.

### Interview Structure
A typical interview day includes:
1. **Program Director Presentation:** An overview of the program's curriculum, culture, and highlights.
2. **Interviews (2 to 4 sessions):** 15-to-30-minute conversations with the Program Director, Associate Program Directors, and attending faculty.
3. **Resident Social (often the night before):** An informal Q&A session with current residents. **Do not let your guard down here.** Residents have a voice in the selection committee, and unprofessional behavior during the social is a major red flag.

### High-Yield Preparation Strategies
* **Master the Behavioral Questions:** Programs utilize Behavioral Event Interviewing (BEI). Prepare stories utilizing the **STAR method** (Situation, Task, Action, Result) for common questions:
  * *"Tell me about a time you had a conflict with a colleague and how you resolved it."*
  * *"Describe a difficult clinical situation and what you learned from it."*
  * *"Tell me about a time you failed and how you bounced back."*
* **Perfect Your "Tell Me About Yourself" Pitch:** This is the icebreaker in 90% of interviews. Keep it under two minutes. Structuring it as **Past** (where you grew up, medical school), **Present** (your recent clinical experiences or research), and **Future** (why this specific specialty and why this program) keeps your answer engaging and professional.
* **Know Your CV Inside Out:** If you listed a case report, be ready to discuss the pathophysiology and clinical course of that patient. If you listed a hobby, be ready to talk about it passionately.

### Thank You Notes and Communication
* **Thank You Emails:** Send a brief, personalized thank you email to your interviewers within 24–48 hours. Mention a specific topic you discussed during your conversation to show active engagement. Keep it short (3–5 sentences).
* **Letter of Intent:** In late January or early February, you should send a single "Letter of Intent" to your absolute #1 program, informing them that you will rank them first on your Rank Order List. Do not send this to multiple programs; the residency world is small, and dishonest ranking promises will ruin your professional reputation.

## 8. Rank Order List (ROL) Strategy

Your Rank Order List is submitted electronically via the NRMP Registration, Ranking, and Results (R3) system. This is the final and most critical step of the matching cycle.

### Core Ranking Rules
* **Rank by True Preference:** The Roth-Peranson algorithm is designed to protect your interests. You should rank programs in order of your *genuine choice*, regardless of where you think you are placed on their list. Ranking a "safe" program first because you think they liked you more than your dream program is a mathematically flawed strategy that can cost you your preferred spot.
* **Only Rank Programs You Want to Train At:** The Match is a **legally binding contract**. If you match into a program on your list, you are legally obligated to train there. If you visited a program and found a highly toxic environment where you would be miserable, do not rank them.
* **The Length of Your List Matters:** Research shows that the probability of matching increases dramatically with the number of programs ranked. For IMGs, ranking **10 to 12 programs** yields a match rate of over 90%. Do everything you can to secure and rank as many programs as possible.

### Key Considerations for IMGs
* **Visa Status:** If you require visa sponsorship, ensure the programs you rank have active visa tracking. If a program verbally promised to sponsor an H-1B but has no history of doing so, evaluate the risk of matching there and being unable to secure your visa in time.
* **Location and Cost of Living:** As an IMG, you may be moving with a family or operating on a tight budget. Consider the cost of living, proximity to international airports, local transit, and cultural communities.
* **Fellowship Match Rates:** If your goal is a subspecialty (e.g., Cardiology, Gastroenterology, Pulmonary/Critical Care), evaluate the program’s historical fellowship placement rates and look for in-house fellowship programs.

### Couples Match
If you and your partner are both applying for residency, you can participate in the Couples Match through the NRMP. You link your Rank Order Lists to create pairs of program options. The algorithm will match you to the highest-ranked pair of programs where both of you are accepted. This requires coordinated scheduling of interviews and extensive logistical planning.

## 9. SOAP / Match Week Backup Planning

If Monday of Match Week arrives and your email reads: *"We are sorry, you did not match into a position,"* you must transition immediately into crisis management. The Supplemental Offer and Acceptance Program (SOAP) is fast-paced, highly stressful, and requires absolute focus.

### What Happens in SOAP?
* **Unfilled List Released:** On Monday morning, unmatched applicants receive access to the list of programs that have unfilled residency positions.
* **Applications Submitted:** You can apply to up to **45 unfilled programs** through ERAS. There are no additional fees for SOAP applications.
* **Programs Review & Interview:** On Tuesday and Wednesday, programs review SOAP applications and conduct rapid, informal phone or virtual interviews.
* **SOAP Rounds:** On Thursday, the NRMP runs multiple "SOAP Rounds." Programs send offers to applicants, and applicants have two hours to accept or reject them. If an offer is accepted, it is legally binding.

### SOAP Strategy for IMGs
* **Be Flexible on Specialty:** Unfilled slots in SOAP are rarely in competitive specialties. The vast majority of open slots are in **Preliminary Internal Medicine, Preliminary General Surgery, Family Medicine, and transitional years**. You must be willing to accept a preliminary year (a 1-year contract) or switch specialties to secure a spot in U.S. clinical training.
* **Do Not Contact Programs Directly:** During SOAP, you are **strictly prohibited** from contacting programs directly unless they contact you first. Any phone calls, emails, or backchannel inquiries initiated by you or your advocates will result in an immediate NRMP match violation and a multi-year ban.
* **Have an Attending Physician Advocate Ready:** While *you* cannot contact programs, your clinical mentors or research advisors (especially if they are prominent U.S. faculty) can contact programs on your behalf to advocate for you. Keep them informed of your unmatched status on Monday morning.

### Backup Options If You Leave SOAP Unmatched
If you do not secure a spot during SOAP, your journey is not over. You must pivot to build a stronger profile for the next cycle:
* **Post-SOAP Vacancies:** Programs occasionally lose matched residents due to visa denials, licensing issues, or personal emergencies in April, May, and June. Monitor databases like Resident Swap, FindAResidency, and AMA vacancy lists for these off-cycle openings.
* **Research Fellowships:** Secure a 1-to-2-year research fellowship at a major U.S. academic institution. This allows you to publish, network, and secure highly influential U.S. letters of recommendation.
* **U.S. Clinical Work:** Work as a clinical research coordinator, medical assistant, or scribe to maintain active clinical exposure in the U.S. healthcare system.

## 10. Timeline: The Complete Cycle

Success in the Match requires executing tasks in a structured sequence. Below is a month-by-month roadmap for a standard Match cycle (based on the **2025–2026 / 2026–2027 timelines**):

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                      PREPARATION                       │
│  Jan - Mar: Register with ECFMG, Study for Step 2 CK   │
└───────────────────────────┬────────────────────────────┘
                            ▼
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                        CLINICAL                        │
│  Apr - Jun: US Clinical Experience, Secure Letters     │
└───────────────────────────┬────────────────────────────┘
                            ▼
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                      APPLICATIONS                      │
│  Jun - Aug: Buy ERAS Token, Write Personal Statement    │
└───────────────────────────┬────────────────────────────┘
                            ▼
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                       SUBMISSION                       │
│  Sep: Submit ERAS (Late Sept), Register with NRMP      │
└───────────────────────────┬────────────────────────────┘
                            ▼
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                       INTERVIEWS                       │
│  Oct - Jan: Virtual Interviews, Attend Socials         │
└───────────────────────────┬────────────────────────────┘
                            ▼
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                        MATCHING                        │
│  Feb - Mar: Rank Order List, Match Week & SOAP         │
└───────────────────────────┘
```

### January – March (Preparation Phase)
* Register with ECFMG and obtain your USMLE ID.
* Finalize your study plan for USMLE Step 2 CK.
* Research and apply for hands-on U.S. clinical electives or externships for the spring and summer.

### April – June (Clinical & Testing Phase)
* Take USMLE Step 2 CK (aim to complete this by June to ensure scores are returned before application compilation).
* Complete U.S. Clinical Experience (USCE) rotations.
* Politely request Letters of Recommendation (LoRs) from your supervising attendings.
* **Late June (typically June 24–25):** ERAS Tokens become available via ECFMG. Purchase your token ($185) to register on AAMC’s MyERAS portal and begin filling out your application.

### July – August (Drafting & Pathway Phase)
* Draft your Personal Statement. Get it reviewed by native English speakers and mentors.
* Request your medical school to upload your official transcript and Medical School Student Performance Evaluation (MSPE).
* Open your **ECFMG Certification Pathway** application. Fulfill the clinical and communication skills requirements (e.g., passing the OET exam and submitting pathway documentation).
* Build your targeted program list using FREIDA, Residency Explorer, and program websites.

### September (Submission Phase)
* **Early September:** Register for the Main Residency Match on the NRMP website.
* Complete all sections of your MyERAS application. Ensure your USMLE transcript release is authorized.
* **Mid-to-Late September:** ERAS begins accepting application submissions. Ensure your application, transcripts, MSPE, and at least three LoRs are fully uploaded and submitted before the designated **late September deadline** (when programs gain access to applications). *Late applications are rarely reviewed.*

### October – January (Interview Season)
* Monitor your email and Thalamus accounts for interview invitations.
* Send follow-up emails to programs of high interest if you have not received news by late November (and have a valid reason, such as updated test scores or a new publication).
* Complete virtual interviews. Send polite, brief thank you notes within 48 hours of your interview.
* Take USMLE Step 3 (highly recommended for non-U.S. IMGs during this time).

### February (Ranking Phase)
* **Early February:** NRMP ranking opens. 
* Draft and submit your Rank Order List (ROL) in the NRMP R3 system.
* Send a single Letter of Intent to your #1 preferred program.
* **Late February:** Rank Order List Certification Deadline. Ensure your list is certified. *An uncertified list will not be matched.*

### March (Match Week)
* **Monday of Match Week:** Unmatched/Matched notification email received. SOAP process begins for unmatched applicants.
* **Monday – Thursday:** SOAP application rounds and interviews.
* **Friday of Match Week:** **Match Day!** Match results are released at 12:00 PM Eastern Time.

### April – June (Transition Phase)
* Receive and sign your official residency contract.
* Initiate the visa sponsorship process (J-1 through ECFMG or H-1B through your matched hospital).
* Complete state training license applications, medical screenings, and hospital onboarding.
* **July 1:** Residency training officially begins.

## 12. Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

### Q1: Can I match without any U.S. Clinical Experience (USCE)?
While it is technically possible for outstanding candidates with exceptional research or high USMLE scores, matching without USCE is **extremely rare**. Over 90% of matched IMGs have at least 1 to 3 months of hands-on U.S. clinical experience. USCE proves you understand the U.S. medical system and allows you to secure the required U.S. Letters of Recommendation.

### Q2: What is the difference between a J-1 and an H-1B visa?
The **J-1 Visa** is an exchange visitor visa sponsored by ECFMG. It is easy to obtain, but it carries a strict **two-year home-country physical presence requirement** (INA Section 212(e)), meaning you must return to your home country for two years after training unless you secure a clinical waiver (e.g., J-1 waiver jobs in underserved areas). The **H-1B Visa** is a temporary worker visa sponsored directly by the employing hospital. It does not have a home-country return requirement and allows for a transition to a green card, but it requires you to have passed USMLE Step 3 before the Match and is only offered by select, typically academic, institutions.

### Q3: Is there a strict cutoff for "Years Since Graduation" (YoG)?
Many residency programs utilize Years Since Graduation as a screening filter to manage massive application volumes. The most common thresholds are **within 3 years** or **within 5 years** of graduation. If you are an older graduate (YoG > 5), you must target programs that explicitly state they do not have a YoG cutoff, and you must offset your YoG with strong, continuous clinical practice or active clinical research in the U.S.

### Q4: How do the ECFMG Pathways work since USMLE Step 2 CS was discontinued?
Since the permanent discontinuation of Step 2 CS, ECFMG has established **six distinct pathways** to evaluate clinical and communication skills:
* **Pathway 1:** For applicants already licensed to practice medicine in another country.
* **Pathway 2:** For applicants who passed an objective clinical skills exam for licensure in another country.
* **Pathway 3:** For graduates of medical schools accredited by an agency recognized by the World Federation for Medical Education (WFME).
* **Pathway 4:** For graduates of medical schools accredited by an agency that has received a determination of comparability by the National Committee on Foreign Medical Education and Accreditation (NCFMEA).
* **Pathway 5:** For graduates of medical schools that issue degrees jointly with a U.S. medical school accredited by the Liaison Committee on Medical Education (LCME).
* **Pathway 6:** For applicants who do not meet the criteria for Pathways 1–5, requiring direct evaluation of clinical encounters by licensed physicians using structured ECFMG assessment tools.
* *Note:* All pathways also require a passing score on the **Occupational English Test (OET) Medicine exam** to fulfill the communication skills requirement.

### Q5: How much does the entire residency application process cost for an IMG?
The process is an expensive investment. A typical applicant applying to 100 programs in a single specialty should expect to spend **$4,000 to $7,000+ USD**. This includes:
* USMLE Exams (Step 1 and Step 2 CK): ~$1,100 to $1,300 each (plus international testing fees if taken outside the U.S.)
* ERAS Token: $185
* USMLE Transcript Transmission Fee: $80
* MyERAS Application Fees (for 100 programs): ~$2,430
* NRMP Registration: $85
* ECFMG Pathway Application: ~$925
* OET Exam: ~$450
* Additional costs for travel, lodging, or clinical rotation fees.

### Q6: If I get a "Preliminary" position, what does that mean?
A **Preliminary position** is a one-year contract, typically in Internal Medicine or General Surgery. It provides you with one year of clinical training but does **not** guarantee a categorical position to complete your residency. If you match into a preliminary year, you must apply to the Match again in the upcoming cycle to secure a PGY-2 position (either categorical or advanced) to finish your residency.

### Q7: Can I apply to programs in different states even if I signaled a specific region?
Yes. Signaling a geographic region in ERAS indicates a strong preference, but it does not prevent you from applying to programs outside that region. Programs outside your signaled region will still receive your application, though they may prioritize applicants who signaled their specific region. If you have a strong reason to apply widely, go ahead, but focus your extra applications on historically IMG-friendly programs.
