# USMLE Step 2 CK Guide for IMGs

*The most important scored exam for IMGs in the pass/fail era — everything you need to know to register, prepare, and score competitively.*

---

## 1. What is USMLE Step 2 CK?

USMLE Step 2 CK (Clinical Knowledge) is a one-day exam that evaluates your clinical knowledge and ability to apply medical concepts in patient care scenarios. It focuses on what a physician should know to provide safe, competent care under supervision.

Step 2 CK is now **the single most important scored exam** for IMGs applying to US residency programs. Since Step 1 went pass/fail in January 2022, Step 2 CK is the **primary numeric metric** program directors use to compare applicants. According to the 2024 NRMP Program Director Survey, **83% of program directors consider Step 2 CK scores** when evaluating applicants, and roughly 30% of programs use a minimum score for interview screening.

For IMGs specifically, Step 2 CK carries even more weight. With no Step 1 numeric score to offset concerns about foreign medical education, your Step 2 CK score becomes the primary proof of your clinical knowledge and competitiveness.

## 2. Eligibility and Registration for IMGs

### How IMGs Register

Like Step 1, IMGs register through **FSMB**:

1. **Log into your ECFMG/MyIntealth account**
2. **Apply through FSMB** at [fsmb.org](https://www.fsmb.org)
3. **Pay the exam fee** — $695 (2026–2027) + **$235 region fee** if testing outside the US/Canada
4. **Receive your scheduling permit** with a three-month eligibility period
5. **Schedule at Prometric** via [prometric.com](https://www.prometric.com)

### Prerequisites

- You **must have passed Step 1** before taking Step 2 CK
- You must be enrolled in or graduated from a WDOMS-listed medical school meeting ECFMG requirements

### Eligibility Period

You will receive a **three-month window** in which you must sit for the exam. A one-time extension is available for a fee.

## 3. Exam Format and Content

### Exam Structure

Step 2 CK is a **one-day examination** divided into **eight 60-minute blocks**, administered in a single 9-hour testing session. Each block contains up to 40 questions, with a maximum of **318 total questions** across the exam.

Break time: minimum **45 minutes** (increased by finishing blocks early), plus a 15-minute optional tutorial.

### Content Organization

Step 2 CK is organized along two dimensions:

**Physician Tasks/Competencies:**
- Promoting health and wellness
- Diagnosing diseases/conditions
- Recommending pharmacotherapy or other interventions
- Applying foundational science concepts

**System-Based Content:**
- Cardiovascular (14–16%)
- Respiratory (10–12%)
- Gastrointestinal (10–12%)
- Renal/Urinary (7–9%)
- Nervous/Special Senses (9–11%)
- Musculoskeletal/Skin (7–9%)
- Endocrine (7–9%)
- Reproductive (7–9%)
- Hematology/Oncology (6–8%)
- Immune System (4–6%)
- Behavioral Health (6–8%)
- Multisystem/General (variable)

The content is overwhelmingly **clinical vignette-based** — you will read a patient scenario and answer questions about diagnosis, next best step, management, or complications.

### Format Change Note (May 2026)
As with Step 1, the USMLE is transitioning to shorter blocks in 2026. Check [usmle.org](https://www.usmle.org/step-exams/step-2-ck) for the latest format details if testing after May 2026.

## 4. Scoring

### Three-Digit Score

Unlike Step 1 (pass/fail), Step 2 CK is scored on a **three-digit scale** from 1 to 300.

- **Current passing score: 218** (raised from 214, effective July 1, 2025)
- **National mean: ~250** for first-time US MD test-takers
- **Standard error of measurement: ~6 points**

## 5. What Score Do I Need? (IMG-Specific Targets)

### General Rule for IMGs

IMGs should target **5–15 points above** the specialty averages listed below. The IMG gap is real — it reflects the higher volume of qualified applicants competing for fewer positions allocated to IMGs.

### Specialty-Specific Score Targets for IMGs

| Specialty | Matched Mean (US Seniors) | IMG Target Score | Competitive Range |
|-----------|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Dermatology | 256–260 | 260+ | Extremely competitive |
| Orthopedic Surgery | 255–258 | 258+ | Very competitive |
| Plastic Surgery | 255–258 | 258+ | Very competitive |
| Neurological Surgery | 254–257 | 257+ | Very competitive |

| Diagnostic Radiology | 251–255 | 253+ | Competitive |

| **General Surgery** | 247–252 | 250+ | Competitive |

| Psychiatry | 244–249 | 246+ | Moderate |
| **Pediatrics** | 243–248 | 245+ | Moderate |
| **Family Medicine** | 240–246 | 242+ | Less competitive |

### IMG Score Benchmarks

| IMG Category | Minimum Competitive | Recommended Target |
|-------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| US-citizen IMG, primary care (IM, FM, Peds, Psych) | 240–245 | 248+ |
| US-citizen IMG, mid-competitive (Anesthesia, EM, Surgery) | 245–250 | 252+ |
| Non-US-citizen IMG, primary care | 245–250 | 252+ |
| Non-US-citizen IMG, mid-competitive | 250–255 | 255+ |

### Academic vs. Community Programs
- **Academic/university programs**: Expect scores 5–10 points above specialty averages
- **Community programs**: Accept scores at or slightly below averages
- **Top-tier programs** (Mayo, MGH, Hopkins): Expect upper 250s–260s regardless of specialty
- **Geographic factor**: Programs in New York, California, and major metro areas often require 10–15 points above published averages due to high application volume

## 6. Study Strategy and Timeline

### Recommended Study Period

- **4–8 weeks of dedicated study** for those who have recently completed clinical rotations
- **3–6 months** for IMGs who have been out of clinical training or need significant review

### Study Phases

**Phase 1: Content Foundation (Weeks 1–3)**
- Begin UWorld Step 2 CK QBank — 1–2 blocks per day
- Use First Aid for Step 2 CK or Step-Up to Medicine as reference
- Focus on high-yield systems: Cardiovascular, GI, Respiratory, Renal
- Review IM topics comprehensively — they dominate the exam

**Phase 2: Intensive QBank (Weeks 4–6)**
- Complete 3–4 blocks of UWorld per day (40 questions per block)
- Review every explanation thoroughly — the educational objectives are gold
- Track performance by subject and identify weak areas
- Supplement weak subjects with targeted resources (e.g., Divine Interventions podcasts for biostatistics and preventive medicine)

**Phase 3: Self-Assessment and Refinement (Weeks 7–8)**
- Take NBME self-assessments every 3–4 days
- Review incorrect answers and identify patterns
- Target weak subjects with focused review
- Take UWorld self-assessment exams
- Review Shelf Exam materials if available

**Phase 4: Final Push (Last week)**
- Rapid review of high-yield topics
- Review biostatistics, ethics, and quality improvement concepts (commonly tested and easy points)
- Take one final NBME 3–4 days before exam
- Do not learn new material — consolidate what you know

### What Makes Step 2 CK Different from Step 1

- **Clinical reasoning over mechanisms.** Step 1 asks "why" (mechanism). Step 2 CK asks "what next" (management).
- **Next best step questions.** Learn the algorithm — when to order imaging, when to biopsy, when to treat empirically.
- **Management questions.** Know first-line, second-line, and contraindicated treatments for common conditions.
- **Prognosis and complications.** Know what happens if you do nothing and what complications to watch for.
- **Ethics and communication.** A meaningful portion covers shared decision-making, informed consent, and difficult conversations.

## 8. Test Day Tips

### Before the Exam
- Confirm your Prometric appointment 1 week in advance
- Verify ID documents match your scheduling permit exactly
- Get 7–8 hours of sleep for at least 3 nights before
- Do not cram the night before — do a light review and relax

### During the Exam
- Arrive 30 minutes early
- Pace yourself: ~1.5 minutes per question
- Use the mark feature for uncertain questions — come back if time allows
- Take all your breaks — stretch, eat, hydrate
- You have 45+ minutes of break time — use it strategically
- For long clinical vignettes, **read the last sentence first** to know what you are being asked before reading the scenario

### After the Exam
- Results typically available in **3–4 weeks**
- You will receive an email notification
- Check your MyIntealth/FSMB account for the score report
- If you pass with a competitive score — proceed with confidence to ERAS applications
- If your score is below your target for competitive specialties — adjust your application strategy (broader applications, target less competitive specialties, consider a gap year for research/USCE)

## 9. Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

### ❌ Treating Step 2 CK Like Step 1
Step 1 tests mechanisms; Step 2 CK tests management. If you try to memorize every biochemical pathway, you will waste time. Focus on clinical decision-making.

### ❌ Skipping Ethics and Biostatistics
These topics appear on every exam and are often the easiest points if prepared. Many students skip them because they feel "obvious" — they are not. Study them specifically.

### ❌ Not Doing Enough Practice Questions
Some students spend too much time reading and not enough time doing questions. Aim for **2,000–3,000 practice questions** minimum before exam day. Questions are your best teacher.

### ❌ Ignoring Weak Subjects
If you are strong in cardiology but weak in renal, your overall score will suffer. Track QBank performance by subject and dedicate extra time to your weakest areas.

### ❌ Taking the Exam Before Ready
It is better to delay and score well than to rush and underperform. For IMGs, a low Step 2 CK score is very difficult to overcome. Use NBME self-assessments to gauge readiness — if you are not consistently hitting your target, push your date back.

### ❌ Not Understanding the IMG Score Gap
IMGs need higher scores than the published matched means for equivalent competitiveness. Targeting 5–15 points above specialty averages is not overreach — it is the data-driven benchmark.

## 11. FAQ

### Q: Is Step 2 CK required for ECFMG Certification?
**Yes.** Passing Step 2 CK is required for ECFMG Certification. Since 2021, Step 2 CK replaced Step 2 CS as part of the pathway requirements for ECFMG Certification.

### Q: What is a good Step 2 CK score for an IMG?
It depends on your target specialty. For primary care specialties (IM, FM, Peds, Psych), aim for **248+** as an IMG. For more competitive specialties (Surgery, Anesthesia, EM), aim for **252+**. For highly competitive specialties (Derm, Ortho, Plastics), aim for **258+**.

### Q: Can I retake Step 2 CK if I pass but want a higher score?
**No.** Once you pass a USMLE Step exam, you cannot retake it for score improvement. This makes your first attempt critical — prepare thoroughly before scheduling.

### Q: Should I take Step 2 CK before or after applying to residency?
For IMGs, it is ideal to have a Step 2 CK score **before** ERAS applications are submitted (typically September). Many IMGs submit applications without Step 2 CK, but this puts you at a disadvantage. Programs may not review your application until your Step 2 CK score is available, delaying consideration.

### Q: How long should I study for Step 2 CK as an IMG?
If recently in clinical training: 4–8 weeks. If out of training for a while: 3–6 months. Use NBME self-assessments to gauge readiness before scheduling.

### Q: Does the exam include CCS (Clinical Case Simulations)?
**No.** CCS is part of Step 3, not Step 2 CK. Step 2 CK is entirely multiple-choice questions based on clinical vignettes.

### Q: How much does Step 2 CK cost for IMGs?
The 2026–2027 application fee is **$695** plus a **$235 region fee** if testing outside the US and Canada. Rescheduling fees apply within 30 days of your appointment.
